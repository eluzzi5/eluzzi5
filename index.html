About Me


I'm fascinated by emerging technologies and their impact on individual users as well as society at large. I graduated from Tufts University with a B.S. in Cognitive and Brain Sciences and minors in Computer Science and English.


My interdisciplinary background as well as my previous work in both academia and startup spaces inspired my interest in human-technology interaction. I am fascinated by the way that technology shapes human behavior and changes the way people interact with one another. I hope to continue my education and learn how to create technology that is not only pleasant to interact with, but can spark lasting positive change – and do so with ethics and wellbeing in mind. I am hopeful for a future in which the "Screen Time" on our phones is a small number compared to "Outdoor Time" or "In Person Conversation Time."

Boston’s cold this time of year
but on my daily cement march
the rain makes the blown up
cancer cells shine. I know you’re tired
of the under-salted food and
the solitary walk from the library.
I hope you know your existence
is striking. Cry not on those wet strolls
through the systematically bathed grass.
Awash in the loss, you are still
moving people with the brilliance
of your seaweed eyes. You are moving me.
You will learn how to run your hands,
all their soft and calloused parts,
through the thick of it.
I feel this too, though it doesn’t make me sad.
I have been swirling in this brackishness,
salt crystals making my abdomen tingle
with pleasure from time to time.
But as the rainy season has come
the salt has washed away.
Not all of it, I’m sure.
It goes something like this:
relishing in songs of religion,
though belonging to none;
counting the people you’d call up for tea
and reaching a meaningless sum;
cars creating artificial wind
blending with your lips holding
onto the whisper
“I am love. I am love.” in a low hum.


When I was young, it was with my legs
jumping on the wooden seesaw swing
among the first streams of daytime light.
Orienting my body in time to the pendulum,
in rhythm with the lake waves floating timidly
out of my periphery

Later I learned to speak with my hands,
like leaves falling slowly on concrete:
I don’t know how to help but I am sorry,
or like violin strings snapping and fanning out:
I don’t know how to help and I am frustrated

When I don’t want to be seen I speak with my eyes
needing, wanting to leave
trying to hug you with the inside of my body
trying to summon your gaze with the yearning in mine

Now with my mouth, each day coated in coffee
I tell myself I no longer have the energy to be weary,
or with my jaw jerking up and down slightly
like it is being held by a dozing puppeteer
I hush the urge inside me to mistakenly misspeak

Though sometimes, like an unwatched pot overflowing,
I pour the steam from my mouth
only for the air to whip it back, a guilty wind,
and I return to speaking the way I have always known:

removing one by one each bodily messenger
my arms, my legs, my eyebrows and lips
slinking up the stairs like an out-of-sync army
leaving a patch of absence to do my communicating

I came into the world already nostalgic for it.
Sturdy metal, slightly rugged and reminiscent
of fishing on a storm-torn boat:
this is the form, previously unquestioned, of the shrunken scallop scepter.

I’ve noticed the salt and pepper speckles
and the brininess of each formerly pristine crease.
I’ve had this scepter for so long that it’s defining characteristic
is belonging to me.

I never considered the rich blue waves
that bore it. I never thought of the people
who’d had it before me; all the times it was shoved
into bags or dropped on hardwood

or held in a hard clench or in the bed of a tender palm,
all the etchings traced by other fingers,
all the shelves where it collected dust,
all the windows it sat by and faded.

Beneath the erosion are signs of the former state,
like years of waves grabbing at the sand and digging up a shoreline.
I entered as a point on its timeline, grabbed hold of the current
and was pushed endlessly forward.

It seems too late now to wonder.
I cannot imagine that it may have been
floating beneath another moonlight
on water that is
out of reach.

There is a bike room in my apartment building
that looks like claustrophobia
and I used to write letters against the egg-white walls
when I’d be on my way out and remember
I had something to say,
and down the hallway there’s a table with a jar of mints.
I used to see how many I could eat at once without anyone noticing
when my parents told me to come say goodbye to our guests
but really they all stood in the hallway,
straddling between a visit and an exit,
and I never understood why they held onto
coming-over-for-dinner
so tightly.
The mints taste like exhaustion so I don’t eat them anymore
but sometimes I check the drawer beneath them
and every once in a while there’s something new there.
I have a pencil box in my room filled with things I’ve found –
scrap leather, a pin, a receipt and a thimble – I don’t know
who they belonged to and I don’t give it much thought
but I like the way they all seem to make sense together.
Past the hallway and out the door
there are street lamps of red, yellow and white.
For the past few years, I’ve almost
never walked there without wearing headphones
and three songs down the street
is a bakery with the most mediocre cupcakes I’ve ever had
but I sat there once with someone who I didn’t know
would become my best friend
and now whenever I taste chemical frosting
I think about how our teeth stained blue
as we desperately tried to fill the speechless spaces
like stuffing socks into a drawer.
If you walk down a little more
there’s a tree that smells like dead fish for most of spring
but there’s one day where it smells
like rain and peaches
and then the next day it dies.
When I miss home I wish I had a bottle of that smell.
Around the corner is the same barbershop
I’ve gone to since I was a kid.
My hair has been as long as
down to my chin and as short as
suitable for an office building
and I’m not sure which I like better.
Down the block there’s a bench made of wood
with dark green paint on the armrests
and I remember when it used to be shiny.
Over the years it’s been my bench and our bench and the bench.
And when I pass by, it sometimes feels like a shot
you expected to hurt
but when the needle goes in
you don’t feel anything.


I remember the winter we spent in Belleayre.
Mornings were spent shivering on a mountain, hands curled
up and red. Evenings were spent by coal fire,
listening to bluegrass on the cd player. Morning snow
dusted the bridge in the backyard. Our snow boots would reveal the wood
and we’d follow it to get drinking water at the stream.

Passing through the bow windows or the layers of steam from coffee, streams
of January sunshine came to remind us we were in Belleayre.
I wanted to bask in it but I would tap my fingers nervously on the wood
counter, remembering the leaves that had shriveled and curled up.
No matter how strong the beams, they did not turn the ice to snow.
Shards of it in the wind still stung the face as hands hovered near the fire.

This was the winter when I learned of the powerlessness of fire.
As much as my skin warmed, like the deposit of a dried up stream,
the cold put something deep inside me. The snow
outside my steps now knows nothing of the snow in Belleayre.
We stand outside my steps now, or lie on my bed, curled
close enough to see your face’s creases, aging the same way as wood.

I remember the winter and know that if I were cold you would
tell me, the color of rosewood glistening in your eyes, to sit by the fire.
I know this because I remember all the times I’d lay there, curled
up in a flannel blanket, like a frog pulled out of a stream.
Some nights I’d warm up enough to love Belleayre,
and the postcard collection in the living room, and the carpet that matched the snow.

Sometimes when I told you this you didn’t get it. You’d tell me there’s fresh snow
and I knew for you that was explanation enough, and you would
walk out to load the ski gear into the car. I think this is when I knew I didn’t love Belleayre
but I loved the things that all happened to reside close together there, like kindling in a fire.
On days when I wanted to stay in, take a walk to the stream,
keep track of the warmth, I saw my desires break you. Your words curled

as they hit the air. Sometimes when your hands and mine are curled
I don’t squeeze so tight because I remember how your voice sounded. I looked out at the snow
because it hurt to hear it. Sometimes now my words stream
out because I don’t want to hear the water stop. Sometimes when I see wood
I knock to check if it would make for a good fire.
I feel one dwindling and I know part of it was lost in Belleayre.

I never want to go back to Belleayre.
I don’t want to watch the death of my fire.
For now, I can scrape the pieces off of us to use as wood.
